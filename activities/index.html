Organizations and Activities in CSE

Activities in CSE

Taylor University’s Computer Science and Engineering Department believes that the strongest Computer Scientist and Engineer is defined not only by remarkable competence, but also strong character, interpersonal skills, and leadership through service. Consequently, the CSE strives to present students with a surplus of opportunities to help them engage with the real world in strong community, while growing in their abilities.

Clubs

TWEET

Also known as Taylor Women Engaging in Engineering and Technology, this club allows women in Taylor’s science, technology, engineering, and math (STEM) to participate in fun activities together. They also work to promote STEM to young girls in the Upland community . TWEET’s mission is to provide a community that encourages and unites women in a field where they are are significantly outnumbered.




TUPilots

Taylor University’s TUPilots is Drone Club for drone enthusiasts of all kinds. An official Taylor club, TUPilots has a number of various drones, as well as an insured fly zone, and indoors drone testing space. TUPilots works to educate Taylor students more about drones, while also also striving to learn more about drones themselves. AirPilots is interested not only in flying drones, but also building and programming them, learning more about their aerodynamics, and exploring piloting by AI. If you are interested in joining, contact Calvin Ochs at calvin_ochs@taylor.edu

TrojanSec

Cybersecurity is a major up and coming field in computer science. TrojanSec seeks to provide an outlet for students with an interest in Cybersecurity.


Center for Missions Computing (CMC)

The vision of the Taylor Center for Missions Computing (TCMC) is to discover, develop, disseminate, and deploy computational knowledge and tools to missionaries and through missionaries. Focused on research, education, service, and provision of resources, the TCMC harnesses the computational tools God makes available to missionaries to help fulfill the Great Commission and Great Commandments.

Established in January, 2010, the (TCMC) formalizes 30 years of voluntary service in missions computing by members of the Taylor Computer Science and Engineering Department. An officially-sanctioned Center of Excellence at Taylor, the TCMC engages faculty, staff, and students in supporting the computing needs of the global missions community.

Internships

Through job fairs and faculty assistance, students receive internships. Every student will work a paid internships for at least one summer.

Research

The CSE department works with several other companies, regularly employing students in internships and practicums.



Annual Events

GameJam

A game jam is an intense party where students team up to develop a video game from scratch in a very short amount of time. The TU CSE GameJam is hosted by the Computer Science and Engineering department and Dr. Jon Denning, and run by students at Taylor University. Regardless of major or knowledge of programming, we invite all Taylor students to participate!
Read more about the GameJam from The Echo.



CSE Freshman and Senior Fall Retreat

After a half semester CSE orientation class, new majors go on a weekend retreat with seniors and faculty. While going through sessions designed to help students understand what a career in CSE looks like, students get to know each other better through team building activities, games, worship, and more!

Computer Science Missions Trip

Every other year, the CMC sends out a group of students to Ecuador or Thailand to develop software for missions organizations that don’t have the resources to develop software they need. Get an Ecuador Photo.

CSE Poster Sessions

On several occasions throughout the year, CSE students will print posters to displaying their projects and present them to potential employers from around the area. Check out some projects here!

Students are encouraged to take this opportunity to create and present professional portfolios to these recruiters, often starting the process to getting a job for the next summer, or after graduation.

Capture the Flag

Our Cybersecurity students often compete remotely in a virtual game of Capture the Flag. Students work in teams to defend their program’s “base,” while also attacking other the bases of other teams. Capture the Flag requires unconventional, creative, and strategic thinking. It is this kind of battle of wits that draws many to cybersecurity. In 2016, our teams placed first and third in the competition.

Paintball

CSE students and professors eliminate their stress on the paintball field. What better way to spend an afternoon?
